The kind of compression that occurs during an email attachment is "lossless": it compress the file cleverly without losing information.  For example, if I had a program in some .exe files, and I sent it over email, it could compress it.  But if it lost even one bit, the resulting exe file would not execute again after it was uncompressed on the other side.

This kind of compression does things like looking for common patterns in the file, and replacing them with a token.  A rough example would be if it found "Howdy Neighbor" in a file 20 times, it could replace all the occurances with say $HN1.  And also write a little dictionary in there someplace that says "$HN1=Howdy Neighbor".

When the file gets to its destination it can be uncompressed, and all occurences of $HN1 are replaced with "Howdy Neighbor".  So it was compressed (instead of 14 characters each, it only needed 4, plus the dictionary), but lost no data.

Some formats, like jpeg and mp3. are not lossless.  Depending on the level of compression, some level of data is lost, and so cannot be recovered or reconstructed later.

Rick

Pixelization doesn't clean-up the data: it replaces it.  Imagine taking a picture of a field of flowers, and beginning to add levels or pixelization to the picture.  Soon, you would not be able to tell there were flowers there at all.  You would see a lot of colored blocks.

Or in the other direction, imagine you are looking at a competely randomize bunch of pixes on your screen: a complete screen of truely random static snow.  Pixelizing this, after a while, you would again start to see colored blocks.

What's happening is that it is processing an area of the image, taking (for instance) the average value of that entire area, and replacing all the blocks in that area with the average.  In video processing, this would result in a field of multi-colored flowers being replaced by, for instance, a big greyish-bluish block, with no resemblance to flowers.  In the noise example, it maybe replace all the random pixels with another big greyish-bluish block.  If you did not know beforehand what the original picture was, you couldn't tell whether your pixelized image was really a field of flowers, a chevy, the sky with clouds, or a random bunch of pixels.

It works the same with the sound files.  Taking something as complex as speech, and replacing frequencies with their averages, loses the speech the farther you go, until you head strange fluctuating square waves.  Doing the same to random static will eventually produce the same thing.  And random fluctuating square waves can sound a bit like speech - or at least, you could have fun sharing with each other what you thing they 'sound like'.
