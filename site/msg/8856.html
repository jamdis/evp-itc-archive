Hi Stefan.  I couldn't agree more about relevance and length of message being important 
factors in helping to rule out false positives.  This is the same guideline I have used all 
along for my radio sweep sessions as well.  

Like Bruce, I have noticed the repeat of the word Arthur, air, and also therapist or therapy, 
along with lots of other single words here and there.  However, each of these words in 
some cases has been used in a longer, relevant response.  

My feeling is the same word can be both a 'false positive' and 'intentional communication', 
depending on the context in which it is heard and whether or not it is part of a longer, 
relevant phrase.  

I agree, also... The chances of hearing these individual words is very high and, in most 
cases, should be disregarded.  Less likely, is that they are heard in a grammatically correct 
sentence.  And the chances of output being a false positive becomes even more slim when 
that grammatically correct sentence is coupled with relevance to a proceeding question or 
comment, and/or to the session content overall.

I would suggest to everyone working with this method, or any kind of automated or 
manual sweep, to let 'relevance' be a golden rule, along with 'length.'  Without relevance, 
there is no way to determine if something is a false positive using these methods, 
especially sweep, where the presence of whole words/sentences could have actually come 
from a broadcasting station at the time the session is conducted.

> in fact one has to be cautious as to "false positives" when using this method, especially 
for 1-syllable words like "(h)ear, "air" and "I". Some of the SpeakJet allophones are 
diphthongs, e.g. #22 ("IYRR" like in "Clear, Hear, Year"), #23 ("EYRR" like in "Hair, Stair, 
Repair"), or #30 ("OHIH" like in "Sky, Five, I"). There are only 72 different allophones, so the 
probability that one-syllable words like "(h)ear/here" , "air", and "I" appear, is relatively 
high.
> 
> I don't assume that everything that is received by this method is true communication. 
As with every method, it depends on various factors like meaningfulness, relatedness, 
length of the message, etc., if a message is regarded as a valid EVP.
