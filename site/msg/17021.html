>>   I think part of the animosity between the scientist/skeptic and the
>> paranormalist/ believer, is that they are speaking a different language, and
>> often don’t know it.
>>
>> Both come up with theories.  And these may lead to conclusions.  A
>> scientist frames this as part of the scientific method, and expects a lot of
>> work to be done between the theory and the conclusions.  A believer often
>> starts with a theory,  and then states new things based on that.  The
>> scientist interprets the believer’s statements as the believers
>> ‘conclusions’, and is appalled at the lack of process that got the believer
>> from theory to conclusion.
>>
>> The believer is appalled at the scientist's closed mind.  But in fact, the
>> believer did not mean their statements to actually be conclusions, in the
>> scientific sense.   The believer thinks about their theories, maybe tests
>> them, and more often than not, comes up with new THEORIES.  But by not
>> stating that these are theories, the scientist sees them as conclusions
>> (since they seemed to flow from theory and testing).  And hence, the
>> friction between these camps.
>>
>> So I think both parties share some of the blame.  The scientist assumes
>> the believer understands the scientific method, and is making conclusions
>> from the scientific method, when the believer may really just be detailing
>> more theories.  The believer watches scientific-looking tv shows, uses
>> scientific-looking equipment, and uses scientific-sounding words like
>> ‘theory’ and ‘evidence’, without realizing this casts them into the
>> scientific method in the ears of the scientific community.
>>
>> What to do?  I think if the scientist/skeptic more often asked things like
>> “Are you sure of that, or is that a theory?”, and the paranormalist/
>> believer used the word ‘theory’ a lot more in their proclamations, it would
>> go a long way toward less hostilities.
