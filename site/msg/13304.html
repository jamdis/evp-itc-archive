Gotcha Frank.  It's certainly a good thing that there are checks and balances in that system to try to both protect the child, and also protect the parent and family.  In this case, and as part of the diagnosis, I suspect it would not be, as you said, simply a matter  of what the parent believed, or even what kind of research they were doing, even if they told a social worker or other authority about the voices they were hearing.  To have someone hospitalized for a psychiatric hold (at least in my state) they have to be a danger to themselves or others, or have some other severe condition (the latter I am not so clear on).  So it might be the content of the ITC messages they are reporting hearing: if they were very bad and violent messages, with urgings to perform harm or other bizarre behaviors for example, the authority could determine this is a sign of being in a dangerous mental state, and take action.  Hopefully, this is an extreme example, and not one anyone here will ever encounter.

But telling authorities you are hearing voices, I am not sure either way (if they don't seem dangerous otherwise) what the authorities would do.  It may be as you said: it depends on the expertise of the authority.

Something I forgot to clarify in my last email.... ITC is not a belief system, belief is not a requisite for this research. The question of mental competency can only be answered after evaluation by a qualified psychologist. If a person is neglectful or abusive then a child could be removed. Social workers usually work under the license of a state certified psychiatrist (who is an MD) and has to give the final diagnosis of the subject. The social worker can provide therapy, recommendations and such but cannot certify a person incompetent.
 
Its not as easy as it looks to take a child away from thier mother and father. Technically, everything is supposed to be fair when it comes to this subject but in a lot of cases it is not. That depends on how well connected the social services person is and how good of a lawyer you have. Ultimately, even the psychiatrist has to present evidence and recommendations to a judge who makes it official.
 
To answer the question whether a child would be removed from parents who research ITC; highly unlikely!

I think Misty makes some good points, and I agree that the issue is similar to how one exposes a child to a belief system, such as a religion.  I agree, too, that it will depend on the individual situation, as some will do this in a healthy way, and some may not: for example, we could all probably name some religious cults that have been more harmful than good.  In the ITC world, I suppose a bad situation would be one where a poor ITC researcher (one who thinks they are getting results, but isn't) insists to the child that they can hear spirits. And I suppose a good situation would be as Misty suggested: where an open mind is presented to them. 

And as Frank noted, this likely also depends on the child. And Frank also mentioned something I thought was interesting: mental health.  What is normal for mental health is often defined as what the majority believes.  Since ITC is still not as big a belief system as other religions or belief systems, it could be seen as abnormal by the uninitiated.  And if Social Services were to get involved (depending on how open minded the worker was) it could result in a messy situation for someone who was insisting they could hear voices talking to them over the radio or other devices.
