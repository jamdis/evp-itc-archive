> That's why I use the op amps--SIMPLE!. I can't do math, so there's no way
> to use discrete components. I use a simple op amp function generator to
> creat the triangle wave used for linear sweep. I use the op amps as an
> anmplifier in the random system to amplify the white noise from a
> transistor, and then as a filter to get rid of all but the lowest frequency
> components of the white noise to get a smooth, randomly varying DC voltage
> used to tune the radios.
>
> The radios are voltage tunable, so you have three sections to the box--a
> radio, the sweep, and the audio amplifiers. Sweep is amother name for he
> tuning voltage, also abreviated VT.
>
> So far the MK484 chip used as the tuner, combined with linear sweep is a
> simple and compact system. Most store bought radios, including the radio
> shack models are inconsistent in quality, and are often very noisy(staticy)
> when swept across their tuning range. I got tired of spending 40 bucks for a
> tuner only to have it sound like crap when the box is assembled, that's why
> I'm spending alot of time on making radios. Car tuners were the worst for
> being noisy.
>
> Brief history-I started out using tuner modules form car stereos for the
> box radio. These were swept wiht a random voltage. The result was often so
> noisy that the voices could barely be heard. It did not occur to me to use
> other tuners until Bill Chapple came out with the Radio Shack hack, and I
> was like DAHHHHH! I can use other radios. So at first I sued the whole
> radio, develpoing switching to turn them on a sweep them with an external
> voltage, then finding out how to use the tuner alone, removing the control
> board. Even then the boxes were a crap shoot, as the radios were often very
> poor sounding when swept. So now I'm working on various home made tuners.
>
