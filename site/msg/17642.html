>  I was listening to Tom's very clear recording and decided to put it
> through Audacity
> so I could view the spectrum. Usually recordings have far too much noise
> for this
> to work well, which is why I like the clearer and cleaner recordings many
> people
> call 'Class A'.  What I found got me to thinking and I want to share my
> thoughts.
>
> First, it's helpful to know that I have quite a bit of professional
> experience studying
> spectra - from ULF up through sound and light and way into the region of
> radio
> waves and even cosmic radiation. I have stared at more spectrum analyzers
> over
> the decades than I care to count. So when I realized what I was seeing in
> Tom's
> recording I was a bit surprised I hadn't thought about it before.
>
> Usually when you look at a visual representation of an audio recording, you
> can
> clearly see the noise with the signal superimposed atop it. The higher the
> noise
> level relative to the signal level, the lower the 'signal-to-noise' ratio
> which results
> in recordings that are very hard to hear. In a normal recording, such as
> you might
> make of yourself speaking into a microphone, there is a specific way the
> noise
> band disappears when the signal level gets high enough. But more than that,
>
> this only occurs when the signal is introduced in the early stages of audio
>
> processing - a noisy amplifier for example looks completely different than
> a high level background noise with someone speaking over it. And that's
> what is important here.
>
> What I noticed that got me thinking was that in the spectrum analysis,
> the noise level was reasonable for this type of recording. And it was
> fairly
> constant. It was what I have come to see in these recordings except that
> the signal to noise ratio was a bit better. Meaning the signal was a bit
> clearer initially. When the sound (which seems to be a young girl's
> voice) begins, the noise level rapidly goes down and almost completely
> vanishes while the signal goes high. This is why you can clearly hear
> the voice: the absence of noise as much as the signal level itself.
>
> Now here's what got me going. I realized that this specific waveform
> suggests strongly that the point of influence is not deep inside the
> electronics. Rather, it is at an exterior point, such as where the signal
> first enters the system. And that would imply that something towards
> the outer part of the electronics is being manipulated, and not the
> recording media or anything near it.
>
> This would also explain why voice phenomena can appear on different
> media and on different electronics such as transistor or solid state or
> vacuum tube. AAEVP has suggested, and based on this observation
> I would agree, that the probable point of influence is a junction near
> the a front-end stage of an early amplifier circuit. Every electronic
> system would have this in common.
>
> I have suggested before that I believe that at least some (probably
> the higher quality) EVPs may be due to operator PK influence.
> MicroPK is believed to operate at or near the molecular level as
> evidenced by things such as healing. And healing agents have
> been identified who have many similar traits to PK agents. So
> it follows they may employ similar abilities and use similar
> mechanisms.  This observation is entirely in line with this idea.
>
> Does this mean that in truth no discarnates are communicating?
> In a dramatic demonstration of who I really am, or more to the
> point what I really believe, let me say that I'm not yet sure.
> Maybe they are, and maybe they are not. This evidence does
> not tell us that answer.
>
> This is similar to work in mediumship research. We have shown
> that mediums can receive information they should not know in
> controlled laboratory conditions. But we don't know scientifically
> where this information comes from. Apparitional experients are
> convinced they have seen or heard the dead, but apparitions of
> the living are quite common as well. The more you learn about
> this phenomena, the more you realize that many commoly
> accepted explanations fall short when applied in general.
>
> As I wrote previously, the real issue is that science today
> does  not have the understanding or the tools to properly
> evaluate this data. Science cannot prove or disprove this
> phenomena. Not today. An honest scientist (and skeptic)
> will tell you this is an unknown, an anomaly. And so, as
> folks here know, it comes down to belief in the absence
> of any protocol or experiment to clearly demonstrate the
> source of these sounds.
>
> But I for one feel comfortable that higher quality recordings
> will help move us along, if for no other reason than we can
> eliminate some variables. The analysis I mentioned can't
> be done on noisier recordings; it took a very quiet one to
> be able to see this clearly. And that's what triggered my
> thoughts.
>
> I also noticed a tiny spike before and after the higher
> quality speech. To some this might suggest an edit,
> but in this case I don't think that happened - here's why.
> You can see the signal emerging from the noise when
> noise is still present and only when it achieves a certain
> level is the noise suppressed. Had this been an edit,
> the noise level on this close a scrutiny would have
> been a bit more obvious - unless the editor got very lucky.
> But in consideration of how the voice appears and
> also the fact that similar low-level spikes appear
> elsewhere in the recording, it would seem more likely
> that the spikes are artifacts of the tuning that's going
> on or some other random events.
>
> Once again, this makes the case for me at least
> for trying to get the highest quality possible. These
> are the kinds of recordings which scientists may be
> able to use sooner than later as understanding and
> tools inevitably improve.
>
> One last thought regarding the notion of PK as an
> explanation. It is very important we come to understand
> PK well. Only then might it actually be used in a
> practical way. I'm sure you've heard or thought about
> a world where you could literally turn things on and off
> with your mind. Or type simply by thinking about it.
> Believe it or not, one way forward to such technology
> could well be through the understanding of PK. So much
> of Parapsychology is actually exploring the leading edge
> (some might say 'bleeding' edge) of science. It shows us
> where things might go and, frankly, that is one interesting
> world at least for me. That's why I have been in the field
> for many decades.
>
> Apologies for the long post, but I thought some might
> find it useful and informative.
