At 60 Hz in copper, the skin depth in copper is around 8.47 mm:

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skin_effect

This means that the current essentially flows through the entire copper wire
at 60 Hz until you get to a diameter of the conductor greater than about 3/4
of an inch.

Also, the table shown in the reference I listed before (
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_conductivity) shows salt water to be
an "OK" conductor, but far less than metals. I actually did some tests with
some instrumentation I was designing a few years ago, comparing distilled
water, tap water, salt water and water recovered from a flooded coal mine.
The conductivity of the water from the mine was on the order of the salt
water's conductivity (which was what we were hoping for).

>   Silver is the best conductor known, with a conductivity of 63.01 E06
> Siemens per meter. Copper is next best at 59.6 E06 S/m. Gold is not the
> best, at 45.2 E06 S/m. Aluminum is much farther down, at 37.8 E06 S/m.
>
> Silver and gold are expensive. They are usually used to plate RF assemblies
> where the skin effect takes place (current doesn't flow uniformly through a
> conductor at RF frequencies but mainly travels at the surface of the
> conductor). Gold has the advantage that it never tarnishes or corrodes with
> most things it is exposed to. It's used a lot in contacts for rotary
> switches and relays.
