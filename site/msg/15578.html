Unfortunately, the examples used were problematic.

You might notice they either played the EVP, then told the audience what the they were supposed to hear, and then played it again.  Or told the audience what they were supposed to hear first.  They never just played the sound (without the leading questions), and gave the audience some time to decide what it was saying on their own.

So while the history at the start was interesting, all the examples were, sadly, more examples of how people can fool themselves with  pareidolia, than examples of spirit communications.


Note also, this also explains why prerecording noises, and using them later for the source of a Q/A session, still works.  It's a tricky explanation when trying to explain how spirits are 'changing' the sounds at session time.  But it is an easy explanation that it wouldn't matter whether the sounds were live or recorded, if it was just people hearing what they wanted to hear.

I realize that many people aren't out to prove anything, and that's fine: but in this case, it seemed a point of the interview was to demonstrate the efficacy of the box.  In this case it would be better to either state they do not expect their examples to be prove anything, or if they expect to convince an audience, to use better testing of their results, and less leading of the audience.

imho
