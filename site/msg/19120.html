It is a simple concept as to why the vents cut down on feedback.  As a
former live sound engineer, feedback was quite the nemesis.

If I remember reading right, you have a small microphone and a small speaker
in your "echo chamber."  A feedback loop happens when a speaker produces
audio and a microphone pics that audio up.  The thing is, if the speaker and
microphone are not far enough apart from one another, a feedback loop occurs
where the audio loop between microphone to speaker keeps building up and
frequencies start to converge into feedback.

Considering that your echo chamber is right under the batteries, the
microphone in the echo chamber is probably picking up the audio from the
main speaker output at the same time as the output that is in the echo
chamber.  The two sources picked up at the same time is causing the
feedback.   By venting the battery chamber, the soundwaves from the main
speaker  are being released easier from the hollow design of the box.  This
allows for the natural reverberation caused by speaker output to stop
reverberating around inside the box, it is this bouncing around of audio
that is= eventually picked up by your echo chamber microphone at the same
time and becomes feedback.

The same thing happens during a concert when a performer gets his microphone
to close to the floor monitor.  A feedback loop occurs because the singer
hear's his voice in the floor monitor at the same time as it is coming out
of his mouth.  A loop occurs where the frequencies build upon each other and
feedback happens.  In your case, the microphone inside the echo chamber is
simply picking up the main speaker at the same time as the speaker in your
echo box, causing the same audio pattern.

Hope this helps,
