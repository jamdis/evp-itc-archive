> > From my experience with all kinds of cameras, I say it's best to experiment. On one of my experiments, I went to a purported haunted alley and took 4 different shots using 4 different cameras from the same angle, taking the photos one after the other within a minute's span. The 4 cameras I used were: 1. Moditronics' so-called "Full Spectrum" (which I've dubbed "Duo-Spectrum" because it only sees into the near UV and IR spectrums, excluding the visible spectrum altogether) and 2. Moditronics "Deep IR" which only sees into the IR spectrum, excluding the visible and UV spectrums, 3. a "Full Spectrum" camera, which sees all 3 spectrums (i.e., UV, visible and IR) and 4. a regular digital point n' shoot consumer camera. The results were interesting. In the pictures I shot with Moditronics' Full ("Duo") Spectrum camera and "Deep IR" camera, I got a face floating in a fire escape from the side of a building adjoining the alley; that face did not show up on
>  neither the full spectrum camera nor on the point n' shoot camera. Interesting indeed. I intend to carry on with more experiments of this type, i.e., shooting the same scene using multiple cameras with different capabilities. BTW, as you may already know, the Moditronics camera takes stills as well as video, and it records onto SD cards.

> > Draiga - if you decide to make videos with one of the SpecterCam cameras, and you want sound, be sure you also have a digital voice recorder running at the same time as DVR cameras record only images, and not sound. Also, about Moditronics' full spectrum and deep IR cameras, you'll need an external light source if you film at night since those cameras do not see into the visible spectrum and if it's a pitch black or very dark environment, they won't see anyhing without a light source. However, they're perfect for daytime ghost hunting, which I do as well as night-time investigations (I don't believe that ghosts only come out and show themselves at night).
