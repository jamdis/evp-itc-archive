> Is it possible to include an echo chamber section that is separate from the
> internal component section of your box?  A box connected to your box but is
> separated by thick wood?  This may cut down on your problem of portability,
> but may not entirely get rid of the problem because waves are so large that
> they conduct through wood (put your ear to the back of an electric guitar or
> bass and strum it, you will still hear a little).  If you attached your
> chamber to the box, had it separated by solid wood, and gave it ventilation
> slits, you might not have this problem anymore.
>
> The demonic sound is not surprising.  Most commercial reverbs allow for
> pre-delay (the sound and then the reverb) in milliseconds, and decay (how
> long the sound lasts after).  Your echo chambers don't have this so if you
> build it on a big board, it is like the reverb is equivalent to yelling in a
> concert hall versus if your echo chamber was built on a smaller board it
> would sound like yelling in a bathroom.  The safe effect, only the result is
> curtailed.
>
> The original reverb plates which are kind of like your current echo chamber
> were built in a wooden box that had limited width but were significant in
> length and height.  Inside of the box were two metal plates.  The sound was
> fed into the box and the amount of reverb was controlled by turning a wheel
> that moved the plates closer to each other or farther apart, and this
> controlled the amount of the reverb.
>
> Your boxes will always sound better with reverb, it is like singing in the
> shower.  Because of the reverberation the voice sounds fuller.

>>> It is a simple concept as to why the vents cut down on feedback.  As a
>>> former live sound engineer, feedback was quite the nemesis.
>>>
>>> If I remember reading right, you have a small microphone and a small
>>> speaker in your "echo chamber."  A feedback loop happens when a speaker
>>> produces audio and a microphone pics that audio up.  The thing is, if the
>>> speaker and microphone are not far enough apart from one another, a feedback
>>> loop occurs where the audio loop between microphone to speaker keeps
>>> building up and frequencies start to converge into feedback.
>>>
>>> Considering that your echo chamber is right under the batteries, the
>>> microphone in the echo chamber is probably picking up the audio from the
>>> main speaker output at the same time as the output that is in the echo
>>> chamber.  The two sources picked up at the same time is causing the
>>> feedback.   By venting the battery chamber, the soundwaves from the main
>>> speaker  are being released easier from the hollow design of the box.  This
>>> allows for the natural reverberation caused by speaker output to stop
>>> reverberating around inside the box, it is this bouncing around of audio
>>> that is= eventually picked up by your echo chamber microphone at the same
>>> time and becomes feedback.
>>>
>>> The same thing happens during a concert when a performer gets his
>>> microphone to close to the floor monitor.  A feedback loop occurs because
>>> the singer hear's his voice in the floor monitor at the same time as it is
>>> coming out of his mouth.  A loop occurs where the frequencies build upon
>>> each other and feedback happens.  In your case, the microphone inside the
>>> echo chamber is simply picking up the main speaker at the same time as the
>>> speaker in your echo box, causing the same audio pattern.
>>>
>>> Hope this helps,
