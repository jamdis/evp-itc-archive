>  Since we're talking about experiments and protocols here are some ideas
> to improve
> the protocol RJ suggested initially. Using a test target is one way of
> establishing a
> truth in time, which is the basis of this experiment. But there are a
> couple of issues.
>
> It is quite possible that someone could discern the target through remote
> viewing alone.
> This would negate the need for involvement of a spirit communicator or
> discarnate entity.
> Outcomes depend on the word of those reporting. The true skeptic (not
> cynic) would
> therefore say that this experiment contains a basic flaw in that it does
> not uniquely
> indicate that the information was obtained in the prescribed manner.
> Providing access
> to the raw audio file would not necessarily constitute proof either, since
> many of these
> communications can only be heard by certain people,
>
> If the audio file were quite clear, timing  becomes an issue. In other
> words, when was
> the audio actually recorded?  This issue can be mitigated simply by
> requiring any
> claimant to email the audio file to a trusted party before the test ends.
> It would be best
> if the trusted party were not otherwise involved in the experiment and it
> was  their duty
> simply to collect the claims. Once all claims had been collected they could
> be posted
> publicly for review by the community in general.  The experimenter could
> decide if any
> of the claims seemed to have hit, and this decision could be reviewed by
> anyone if desired.
>
> The important detail however is that this experiment does not completely
> answer the
> question. It is a first attempt and certainly can be improved on successive
> trials.
>
> Successive trials are always a good idea, since it provides replication.
> And it would be
> best if others replicated the experiment using a very similar protocol. If
> these were to
> succeed, the community could then state that out of X trials Y had
> succeeded (by
> some definition of success) potentially giving a success rate above that
> expected
> by random chance. This would make the outcome quantifiable and measureable
> and thus meaningful.
>
> But also important to note, this would only prove the potential veracity of
> the messages.
> It would not (and cannot as far as I can tell) prove the origin of the
> messages. This detail
> will, I fear, remain a matter for belief and speculation for quite some
> time to come.
