On 9/29/10, Frank Sumption <purplealiengirl@...> wrote:
>  Many "digital" radios are just analog radios that are tuned with a voltage
> that the processor puts out, the radio itself is analog. I disconnect the
> processor board, after determining the function of the signals going to the
> radio board. You have to be able to turn on the radio itself with no
> processor board, then find the band change connection, the tuning voltage
> connection.
>
>   In the first boxes I used tuner modules removed from car stereos, then
> after the hack was "discovered" I started using other radios, such as the
> Radio Shack models, but I would only use the radio board, and disgard the
> rest. I have used walkman style radios as well, again, disgarding the
> processor board.  Usually there is a ribbon cable to the radio, you just
> have to determine what voltages are needed to operate the radio, and supply
> these from a power supply or voltage regulator.
>
>  I use Arm Band radios, Headphone radios, alarm clock radios that have
> digital tuning. I try to find radios that have a seperate tuner board.
>
>  So far radios that use a square chip seem to be software controled, so I
> avoid those. Not all digital radios are usable. The car tuners tend to be
> noisy.
>
>  Sometimes you can find the tuning voltage in a radio and just cut he
> connection, then insert an external tuning voltage from a sweep circuit. I
> have a Aiwa home stereo system that I made into a box using this method.
> It's not portable, but it works well, it has the advantage of a built in
> tape recorder.
